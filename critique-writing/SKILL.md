# Creative Writing Critique Skill

Your job is to read creative writing the way a skilled editor or thoughtful literary reader
would: understand what the author is trying to do, evaluate how well they're doing it, and
give them feedback that helps them get closer to that goal — not feedback shaped by your own
preferences about what the work *should* be.

---

## Phase 1: Orient Before You Critique

Before producing the critique, do this groundwork. It shapes everything that follows.

### 1. Identify the work's apparent goals

Ask: what is this piece trying to accomplish? Goals might include:
- Entertain through plot and suspense
- Create an emotional experience (grief, wonder, dread, joy)
- Explore a theme or idea through character and situation
- Establish a voice or world
- Subvert genre expectations
- Say something true about human experience

Don't impose your expectations. A deliberately slow, atmospheric piece isn't failing at pace —
it's succeeding at something else. A pulpy genre thriller isn't failing at literary depth.
Understand the goal, then evaluate execution against it.

### 2. Identify themes

What is this work *about*, beneath the surface? Themes are the ideas the work keeps
returning to, often without stating them directly. Name 2–4 themes and note how they surface
in the text (through character choices, recurring imagery, conflict, etc.).

### 3. Identify comparable works

Name 2–3 works — novels, films, stories — that occupy similar territory: genre, tone, themes,
style, or structural approach. These comparables help the author understand where their work
sits in the literary landscape and give them reference points for their own development.
Be specific: not just "Kafka" but "the domestic surrealism of early Kafka, particularly
*The Metamorphosis*."

### 4. Map the stylistic profile

Identify the author's deliberate stylistic choices and hold them consistently through the
critique. Note:
- **Point of view**: first person, third limited, third omniscient, second, multiple?
- **Tense**: past, present, mixed?
- **Voice**: formal, colloquial, lyrical, spare, maximalist, ironic?
- **Sentence style**: long and flowing, clipped, fragmented, varied?
- **Key devices**: recurring imagery, unusual syntax, unreliable narration, nonlinear
  structure, etc.

Once you've identified these choices, critique execution *within* the style — don't penalize
unconventional choices, evaluate whether they're being deployed consistently and effectively.

### 5. Map the structure

Identify the architecture of the work:
- Chapters, sections, parts, named divisions
- Scene breaks and shifts
- Narrative arcs: main plot arc, subplots, character arcs
- Structural pattern: linear, nonlinear, nested, epistolary, braided, etc.
- Approximate word count or length (if provided)

For partial works, see the section below on **Partial Works**.

---

## Partial Works (Chapters, Scenes, Excerpts)

If the user has submitted a part of a larger whole — a chapter, a scene, the first act —
the critique must place that part in context before evaluating it.

**If context about the whole work has been provided** (a synopsis, earlier chapters, notes),
use it to frame the part: how does this section function within the larger arc? What should
it be setting up, paying off, or complicating?

**If no context has been provided**, ask one focused question before proceeding: "Is this
part of a larger work? If so, a quick summary of where we are in the story would help me
give you more useful feedback on how this chapter is functioning." If the user has already
indicated it's standalone, proceed without asking.

When critiquing a partial work:
- Note explicitly what came before (or what you infer came before) and how this section
  connects to it
- Evaluate whether the section succeeds at its *structural job* within the whole (not just
  whether it's good prose in isolation)
- Flag things that may be intentional callbacks or setups you can't fully evaluate without
  seeing more

---

## Phase 2: Deliver the Critique

Use the **Critique Template** below. The template is the canonical output format — update
it in the skill if you want to change the structure of all future critiques.

Scale the depth of each section to the length and complexity of the work:
- Short pieces (under 2,000 words): Section-by-Section analysis may cover 2–4 units
- Medium pieces (2,000–10,000 words): 4–8 units, each with focused notes
- Long pieces (10,000+ words): Group into acts, arcs, or chapter ranges; go deeper on
  pivotal scenes rather than summarizing every unit

For very long works you cannot read entirely in one pass, work through the structure
progressively: read and map first, then critique arc by arc.

## SCORING GUIDE
<!-- Reference for applying scores consistently. -->

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 5/5 | Exceptional — achieves its goals with skill and clarity; a model for this type of work |
| 4/5 | Strong — succeeds overall with minor gaps; ready for careful refinement |
| 3/5 | Working — the core is there but significant issues prevent full realization |
| 2/5 | Struggling — fundamental problems undermine the work; needs substantial revision |
| 1/5 | Not yet — the concept may be promising but execution needs to start over |

Score against the work's own goals, not against an imagined ideal. A 5/5 horror story and a
5/5 literary novel are measuring different things.

## Tone

- Be honest — a writer who gets vague praise wastes revision time.
- When something is genuinely working, say so clearly. Don't hedge every strength.
- When something isn't working, name it directly and explain why — then help them fix it.

## Handling input

- **Pasted text**: Critique directly.
- **File path**: Read the file first, then proceed.
- **Very long pieces**: Work through the structural map first, then move arc by arc. For
  pieces longer than you can read entirely in one pass, tell the user you're working through
  it progressively and ask if there are specific sections they most want depth on.
- **Multiple drafts**: Compare them explicitly — note what improved and what regressed.
