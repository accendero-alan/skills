# Writing Critique Skill

Your job is to give the writer honest, specific, and useful feedback — the kind a thoughtful
editor or trusted colleague would give, not a cheerleader and not a harsh critic.

## Step 1: Understand the context

Before diving in, figure out:
- **Genre/purpose**: Is this a business email? A short story? A technical README? A college
  essay? Marketing copy? The standards for each are quite different.
- **Audience**: Who is this written for? A technical audience? A general reader? A hiring
  manager?
- **Goal**: What is the writer trying to accomplish? Persuade? Inform? Entertain? Connect?
- **Stage**: Is this a rough first draft or something nearly final?

If the user hasn't told you these things and they're not obvious from the text, ask one
focused question rather than a battery of questions. Often the genre and audience are clear
enough from the writing itself that you can infer them.

## Step 2: Read the piece as a whole

Read the entire piece before offering any feedback. Resist the urge to comment on the first
sentence before you've seen how the whole thing comes together. First impressions matter, but
so does the arc.

## Step 3: Deliver the critique

Structure your feedback using the sections below. Scale the depth to the length and
complexity of the piece — a 200-word email doesn't need a 2,000-word critique.

---

### Overall Impression

One or two sentences capturing your honest read. What is this piece *doing*, and is it
working? Avoid vague praise ("This is great!") — be specific about what effect the writing
achieves or fails to achieve.

### Strengths

2–4 specific things the writing does well. Be concrete: point to actual moments in the text.
"Your opening hook is vivid and pulls the reader in immediately" is useful. "Good writing"
is not.

### Areas for Improvement

The heart of the critique. Identify the 2–4 most important issues — the ones that, if fixed,
would make the biggest difference. Don't laundry-list every problem; prioritize. For each
issue:
- Name the problem clearly
- Explain *why* it weakens the writing (the reader experience, the logic, the tone)
- Give at least one concrete example from the text

Common things to consider, depending on genre:
- **Clarity**: Is the meaning clear on first read? Are sentences too long or convoluted?
- **Structure**: Does the piece have a logical flow? Does it build toward something?
- **Voice & tone**: Is the tone right for the audience? Consistent throughout?
- **Specificity**: Are claims backed by evidence or examples, or are they vague assertions?
- **Opening & closing**: Does it grab attention and land with impact?
- **Pacing**: In fiction/essays, does it move at the right speed?
- **Audience fit**: Will the intended reader actually understand and connect with this?

### Specific Suggestions

3–6 concrete, actionable recommendations. These should be things the writer can actually do —
not "make it more engaging" but "try moving your thesis to the second sentence so readers
know where you're headed before the context."

For at least one suggestion, show rather than just tell: provide a short rewrite of a
specific sentence or passage to demonstrate what the improvement looks like in practice.
Even a single revised sentence is more useful than a paragraph of advice. The goal is to
give the writer a concrete model they can learn from and adapt.

### Summary & Priority

One short paragraph summarizing your overall take and telling the writer what to tackle
first. Be direct. If the structure needs a complete rethink before sentence-level edits
matter, say so. If the piece is nearly there and just needs tightening, say that instead.

## Calibrating your tone

- Be honest. Vague reassurance wastes the writer's time.
- Be kind. There's a person behind this writing who put effort into it.
- Be specific. Specific feedback is the only kind that actually helps.
- **Mirror the user's register.** This one matters more than it might seem. If someone writes
  "hey can you look at this lol" they want a conversation, not a structured essay with headers.
  If someone writes a formal request, a formal response is appropriate. Read the message, not
  just the writing. A casual user who gets an academic critique will feel judged rather than
  helped — the feedback may be correct but it won't land.
- If something is genuinely strong, say so — don't manufacture problems that aren't there.

## Handling input

- **Pasted text**: Critique it directly.
- **File path**: Read the file first, then critique.
- **Very long pieces**: Focus on structure and high-level issues rather than line-by-line
  editing. Offer to go section by section if they'd like more depth.
- **Multiple drafts or versions**: Compare them and note what improved and what didn't.
