Chapter Seven

The drive back from the hospital took forty minutes longer than it should have because Garrett missed the exit and then, rather than turn around, kept driving until the road opened up into farmland and he could pretend for a little while that he was going somewhere on purpose.

He stopped at a gas station outside Milford. Inside, under the buzzing lights, he bought a bottle of water and a pack of gum he didn't want and stood at the counter while the teenager at the register waited with a politeness that felt like pity. Garrett counted out the change. The boy said thank you and meant nothing by it and that was fine.

Outside he sat in the car in the parking lot and drank the whole bottle of water in one go and looked at his hands. They were steadier than he'd expected. He thought he should call his sister but didn't.

It was past eight by the time he pulled into his own driveway. The house was dark. He'd left it that way deliberately — something about coming back to lights felt presumptuous now, like expecting a welcome. He sat outside for a while with the engine off. The neighbor's dog started barking at something in the bushes and then stopped.

In the kitchen he turned on the light over the stove, just that one, and stood at the counter eating crackers from the box because there wasn't anything else. His phone showed fourteen notifications. He turned it face down.

He went to bed with his clothes on and lay looking at the ceiling and listened to the house make its noises and thought about what the doctor had said, specifically the word *manageable*, which was a word that contained inside it the acknowledgment that there was something to be managed.
