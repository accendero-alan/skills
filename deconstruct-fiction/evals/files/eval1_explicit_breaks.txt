The Last Train

Mara arrived at the station twenty minutes early, which she always did, which her mother had always done, which was the one habit she hadn't managed to lose. The platform was nearly empty. A man in a yellow jacket stood at the far end reading a folded newspaper. She found a bench and sat with her bag between her feet and watched the board flicker through arrivals that weren't hers.

The train to Bristol was delayed. Of course it was.

She pulled out her phone. There were three messages from David, each one shorter than the last, each one a little more carefully worded, which was worse somehow than if he'd just said the thing directly. She put the phone back in her bag without answering.

***

The café inside the station was the kind that had existed forever, or felt like it — brown laminate tables, a display case of sandwiches that looked like they'd been there since the nineties, a woman behind the counter who moved with total unhurried authority. Mara ordered a tea she didn't want and carried it to a corner table.

The man from the platform came in, still carrying the newspaper. He sat two tables away and opened it again, and she realised he hadn't been reading it at all — he was using it as a prop, the way you did when you didn't want to look like you were watching. She didn't know if he was watching her or just watching generally. Either way she felt suddenly very visible.

Her tea was too hot to drink. She wrapped both hands around the cup.

***

When the announcement came it wasn't for her train. It was for a cancellation — the 14:42 to Cardiff, which meant the platform would fill with displaced people looking for seats, looking for information, looking for someone to blame. She watched them through the café window: a family with a pushchair, two teenagers with enormous rucksacks, an older man standing very still with his eyes closed.

She texted David back. Just: *I know.*

Then she picked up her bag and walked out to find the new platform, because the train was coming whether she was ready or not.
