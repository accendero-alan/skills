Part Two, Chapter Three

The morning of the vote, Selin woke before dawn and lay in the unfamiliar hotel bed listening to the city. Istanbul at five a.m. sounded like itself — distant ferries, a muezzin somewhere east, the low-frequency hum of a place that never really slept. She had a speech to give in four hours. She had been giving versions of it in her head for eleven years.

She dressed in the dark so she wouldn't wake Fatma, who slept on her stomach with one arm off the edge of the bed and who had flown in from Ankara the night before without being asked. That was the thing about Fatma. You didn't have to ask.

Downstairs, the hotel lobby was empty except for a young man at the front desk who looked up when she came out of the elevator and then looked away again, either recognizing her or not, she couldn't tell anymore. She took a seat near the window with a glass of tea and watched the Bosphorus go pale.

---

She had been twenty-three when she first testified before the commission. This was in a different city, a different decade. Her mother had pressed her good coat for her and told her to stand straight and look at them directly, don't look at your notes, look at the men. There had been seven men. She had looked at every one.

The commission had thanked her for her testimony and done nothing with it. She had expected this. What she had not expected was the journalist outside, the young woman who had pressed a card into her hand and said, very quietly: *they recorded everything you said. They can't unrecord it.* Selin had kept the card until it fell apart.

---

By six the lobby was beginning to fill — aides, journalists, a television crew setting up near the entrance. Fatma appeared from the elevator in last night's clothes with her hair still wet, carrying both their room key cards for some reason, looking entirely untroubled.

"You should have slept," Fatma said, sitting down across from her.

"I did sleep."

"You slept the way you sleep before things."

Selin wrapped both hands around her tea glass. "Is the car confirmed?"

"The car is confirmed. Eat something." Fatma produced a bread roll from somewhere — Selin didn't ask where — and set it on the table between them. "You're going to win today."

"You don't know that."

"I know it." Fatma said it the way she said most things: with a certainty that had nothing to do with evidence and everything to do with will. "Now eat the bread."
